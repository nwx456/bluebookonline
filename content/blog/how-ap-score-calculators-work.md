**Short answer:** **AP score calculators** weight your **MCQ and FRQ** using official section percentages, build a **composite score**, and map it to **1–5** using historical cutoffs — equating means results are **estimates only**.

## Step 1: Section Weights

Each AP Course and Exam Description specifies MCQ vs FRQ weight. Example:

- **AP Calculus AB:** 50% MCQ / 50% FRQ
- **AP CSA:** 55% MCQ / 45% FRQ

## Step 2: Composite Score

Your raw points are scaled to a maximum composite (e.g. 108 on Calc AB). FRQ parts use rubric max points.

## Step 3: Cutoff Mapping

Historical released data suggests composite ranges for each AP score:

| AP score | Typical composite % |
|---|---|
| 5 | ~65–75%+ (varies) |
| 4 | ~55–65% |
| 3 | ~45–55% |

## Step 4: Equating (What Calculators Cannot Do)

College Board adjusts cutoffs post-exam so a 3 means the same across years. Your May 2026 form may shift borders by a few composite points.
