AP US Government centers on **9 foundational documents** and **15 required Supreme Court cases**. FRQ 3 (SCOTUS Comparison) directly tests this knowledge.

Official list: [AP US Government CED](https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/courses/ap-united-states-government-and-politics) → Appendix.

## The 15 Required Cases

| Case | Year | Key Holding |
|---|---|---|
| Marbury v. Madison | 1803 | Judicial review established |
| McCulloch v. Maryland | 1819 | Necessary and Proper Clause; federal supremacy |
| Schenck v. United States | 1919 | Clear and present danger test (1st Amendment limits) |
| Brown v. Board of Education | 1954 | School segregation unconstitutional (14th Amendment) |
| Baker v. Carr | 1962 | Federal courts can hear redistricting cases |
| Engel v. Vitale | 1962 | School-sponsored prayer violates Establishment Clause |
| Gideon v. Wainwright | 1963 | Right to attorney in criminal cases (6th Amendment) |
| Tinker v. Des Moines | 1969 | Student symbolic speech protected |
| New York Times v. US | 1971 | Prior restraint standard (Pentagon Papers) |
| Wisconsin v. Yoder | 1972 | Free exercise of religion (Amish schooling) |
| Roe v. Wade | 1973 | Privacy right and abortion access (note: Dobbs 2022 overturned) |
| Shaw v. Reno | 1993 | Racial gerrymandering violates Equal Protection |
| United States v. Lopez | 1995 | Limits on Commerce Clause power |
| McDonald v. Chicago | 2010 | 2nd Amendment incorporated via 14th Amendment |
| Citizens United v. FEC | 2010 | Corporate political spending protected (1st Amendment) |

## How to Study Each Case

For every case, memorize:

1. **Constitutional principle** tested (e.g., federalism, civil liberties, civil rights)
2. **Holding** — what the Court decided
3. **Significance** — why it matters for American government today
4. **Connection to CED units** — which unit covers this case

## SCOTUS Comparison FRQ Strategy

FRQ 3 gives a **non-required case** and asks you to compare it with a **required case**:

- Identify the **shared constitutional principle**
- Explain how each case's holding relates to that principle
- Use specific details (not just case names)

Example structure: "Both *McCulloch* and [non-required case] address federal power under the Constitution. In *McCulloch*, the Court held that... Similarly, in [non-required case]..."
