Percentiles translate your SAT score into **"you scored higher than X% of test takers"** — the most useful context for understanding whether your score is strong, average, or needs improvement.

This guide provides 2026 percentile estimates for the Digital SAT, based on College Board score distribution data and recent cohort reporting.

**Key insight:** A 600 in one section is not "average" — it is above average (~65th percentile). Many students assume 600 means "C grade" — on the SAT, it means top third.

## How to Use Percentiles

### For college planning
Look up your target colleges' **middle 50% score ranges** (not percentiles). A college reporting 1350–1480 middle 50% means half of admitted students scored in that range.

### For retake decisions
- Below 50th percentile (under ~1050): Significant room for improvement with structured prep
- 75th–90th percentile (1200–1350): Strong score; retake only if below target college ranges
- Above 95th percentile (1400+): Diminishing returns unless targeting top-10 schools

### For superscore planning
If your sections are imbalanced (e.g., 90th percentile Math, 60th percentile R&W), percentiles show exactly where to focus retake prep. See [superscore strategy](/blog/sat-superscore-explained).

## Digital SAT vs Paper SAT Percentiles

The 400–1600 scale is unchanged. Percentile distributions shifted slightly when the Digital SAT launched in 2024 because:

- Shorter test attracted some different test-taker demographics
- Adaptive format changes score distribution shape at the tails

For practical purposes, **percentiles from this table apply to both formats**.
