A 5 on an AP exam means "extremely well qualified" — roughly equivalent to an A in a college course. Here's how to get there, backed by College Board's own resources.

## Strategy 1: Start with the CED

Download your [Course and Exam Description](https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/courses) from AP Central. The CED tells you:

- Exact exam format and section weights
- Every unit's exam percentage
- Sample questions with scoring notes

Build your study plan around **unit weights**, not textbook chapter order.

## Strategy 2: Know Your Weights

| Subject Type | MCQ Weight | FRQ Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Most sciences | 50% | 50% |
| History | 40% | 60% |
| AP Psych | 67% | 33% |
| AP Lang/Lit | 45% | 55% |
| AP Gov | 50% | 50% |

If FRQs are 50%+ of your score, **half your study time** should be FRQ practice.

## Strategy 3: FRQ Rubric Mastery

For every subject, download **scoring guidelines** from AP Central past exams. Score your own practice FRQs against the rubric — not your teacher's opinion.

- History: [DBQ guide](/blog/ap-european-history-dbq-guide)
- Environmental Science: [FRQ guide](/blog/ap-environmental-science-frq-guide)
- Digital typing: [FRQ strategies](/blog/digital-frq-typing-strategies)

## Strategy 4: Practice Test Cadence

| Weeks Before Exam | Action |
|---|---|
| 8 | Diagnostic full test — identify weak units |
| 6 | Unit-focused MCQ drills |
| 4 | First scored mock + [score calculator](/tools/ap-score-calculator) |
| 2 | Second mock — target weak section (MCQ or FRQ) |
| 1 | Review mistakes only — no new content |

**A 5 comes from consistent partial credit on FRQs + solid MCQ accuracy — not perfection.** Show your work, check conditions, write in context, and practice under timed conditions.
