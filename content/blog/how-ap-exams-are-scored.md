Every spring, r/APStudents fills with the same question: *"Did I get a good curve?"* The short answer from College Board documentation: **AP exams are not curved like a high school test.** They use **composite scoring** and **equating** to convert your performance into the familiar 1–5 scale.

Understanding this process helps you study smarter, interpret practice tests accurately, and use tools like our [free AP score calculators](/tools/ap-score-calculator) without falling for curve myths.

## Step 1: Raw Scores From MCQ and FRQ

Every AP exam has two major scored sections (exact counts vary by subject):

| Component | Typical role | Example (AP Calculus AB) |
|---|---|---|
| Section I: Multiple choice | Often 40–67% of composite | 45 questions, 50% weight |
| Section II: Free response | Often 33–60% of composite | 6 FRQs, 50% weight |

**There is no penalty for wrong answers on MCQs** — College Board scores only correct responses. On FRQs, partial credit is common; show all work even if your final answer is wrong.

For fully digital exams (APUSH, AP Lang, AP Psych, etc.), FRQs are typed in Bluebook. For hybrid digital exams (Calc, Bio, Chem, Physics), you view FRQs on screen but **handwrite answers in paper booklets**.

## Step 2: Building the Composite Score

College Board applies **section weights** from each course's official Course and Exam Description (CED). Your raw MCQ correct count and FRQ points are scaled and combined into a single **composite score**.

Example for AP Psychology (MCQ-heavy):

- 75 MCQs weighted at ~67% of composite
- 2 FRQs weighted at ~33%

Example for AP U.S. History (essay-heavy):

- 55 MCQs at ~40%
- SAQ + DBQ + LEQ at ~60%

## Step 3: Equating (Not a Curve)

**Equating** is the process College Board uses after all exams are administered. If a year's test is harder, cutoffs shift slightly lower; if easier, they shift higher. The goal: **a 3 in 2026 means roughly the same achievement as a 3 in 2024.**

This is why:

- Cutoffs are **not published before July** score release
- Online "curve" posts on Reddit are **speculation** until official data arrives
- You don't need a "good curve year" — you need strong section performance

College Board describes this process in their [AP Exam Scoring overview](https://apstudents.collegeboard.org/view-scores/about-ap-scores/what-is-an-ap-score).

## What Percentage Is a 5?

There is no universal percentage. Historically, many exams map a **5 to roughly 65–75% of total composite points**, but this varies widely:

- **AP Psychology:** Strong MCQ performance can carry you; 5s often need ~75%+ composite
- **AP Calculus AB:** FRQs are half the score; calculator sections matter
- **APUSH / World:** Essay quality (DBQ/LEQ) often determines 4 vs 5

## How to Use This When Studying

1. **Know your section weights** — read the CED for your subject
2. **Practice both sections** — MCQ-only prep leaves half your score on the table
3. **Simulate exam conditions** — timed Bluebook-style practice on [AP Practice Exam Online](/exams)
4. **Track weak areas** — use [Mistake Review](/dashboard/mistakes) after each attempt
5. **Estimate, don't obsess** — calculators give planning estimates, not July guarantees

## Common Myths Debunked

**Myth:** "My test form was harder so the curve will save me."  
**Fact:** Equating happens across all forms nationally; you can't predict it from your room's reaction.

**Myth:** "Leaving blanks is better than guessing."  
**Fact:** No wrong-answer penalty — always eliminate and guess.

**Myth:** "MCQ percentage equals AP score."  
**Fact:** FRQ weighting changes everything. 80% MCQ might be a 3 or 4 depending on essays.
