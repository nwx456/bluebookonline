College Board publishes **score distributions** showing what percentage of students earned each score (1–5) on every AP exam. These numbers — updated each year on [AP Central](https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/score-reports/annual) — are the most reliable data for answering "How hard is this AP?" and "What are my chances of passing?"

This guide summarizes distributions for major exams, explains how to read them, and links to [free score calculators](/tools/ap-score-calculator) so you can connect national trends to your personal practice results.

## How to Read AP Score Distributions

Each row shows the **percentage of all test-takers** who received scores 1 through 5:

| Score | Meaning | College use |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Extremely well qualified | Credit/placement at most colleges |
| 4 | Well qualified | Credit at many colleges |
| 3 | Qualified | Passing; credit varies by institution |
| 2 | Possibly qualified | Rarely earns credit |
| 1 | No recommendation | Does not qualify |

**Pass rate** = percentage scoring **3 or higher** (scores 3 + 4 + 5).

## Highest Pass Rates: What It Means

Exams with pass rates above ~75% often share traits:

- **Self-selected students** (BC Calc, Physics C) — only strong students enroll
- **Heritage speakers** (Chinese, Spanish Language) — native exposure advantage
- **Smaller, motivated cohorts**

High pass rate ≠ easy content. AP Calculus BC covers AB plus series, polar, and vector — it's rigorous; the students taking it are prepared.

## Lowest Pass Rates: Should You Avoid Them?

AP Physics 1 (~41% pass) and AP Environmental Science (~39% pass) show lower qualifying rates. Common reasons:

- Large enrollment including underprepared students
- Conceptual depth + math application (Physics 1)
- Breadth of content (Environmental Science)

Low pass rate doesn't mean "don't take it" — it means **prepare seriously**. Use [subject practice tests](/exams), track progress in [Insights](/dashboard/insights), and estimate your score with our [Physics 1 calculator](/tools/ap-score-calculator/ap-physics-1).

## Using Distributions to Set Goals

1. **Find your exam** in the table above
2. **Identify the 5-rate** — if only 10% earn 5s, a 5 requires top-tier performance
3. **Practice under timed conditions** on [AP Practice Exam Online](/exams)
4. **Plug results into a calculator** — e.g., [AP Bio calculator](/tools/ap-score-calculator/ap-biology)
5. **Review mistakes systematically** via [Mistake Review](/dashboard/mistakes)

## Score Distributions vs. Equating

Distributions show **outcomes**; [equating](/blog/how-ap-exams-are-scored) determines **cutoffs**. A exam with a low 5-rate might have generous equating if the test was unusually hard — you can't know until July.
